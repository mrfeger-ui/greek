Where to Start

Your Learning Roadmap

Modern Greek (Νέα Ελληνικά) is spoken by about 13 million people. Most learners reach conversational fluency in 600–750 hours. Here's how to structure your time.

1

Master the Alphabet

Greek uses its own script — 24 letters. You can learn to read and write all of them in 1–2 weeks. This unlocks everything: pronunciation, vocabulary recognition, and the joy of decoding signs in Greece.

Weeks 1–2
2

Pronunciation & Phonetics

Greek is highly phonetic — words are pronounced exactly as spelled. Focus on the sounds that don't exist in English: γ (guttural g), χ (like Bach), and the double vowel combinations like αι, ει, οι (all sound like "ee").

Weeks 2–4
3

Core Vocabulary (500 words)

Learn the most frequent 500 Greek words — these cover ~80% of everyday speech. Many are already familiar from English: θέατρο (théatro = theater), μουσική (mousikí = music), δημοκρατία (dimokratía = democracy).

Months 1–3
4

Basic Grammar Structure

Greek has grammatical gender (masculine/feminine/neuter), noun cases, and verb conjugations. It's more complex than Spanish, but patterns emerge quickly. Focus on present tense verbs and basic sentence structure first.

Months 2–6
5

Listening & Speaking Practice

Start with Greek music, simple YouTube channels, and podcasts for learners. Apps like iTalki connect you with native speakers for conversation practice — even 30 minutes a week makes a huge difference.

Month 3 onward
6

Immersion & Real Conversation

By month 6, aim to watch Greek TV with Greek subtitles, read children's books, and hold simple conversations. A trip to Greece — or connecting with the Greek community in LA — will accelerate everything.

Month 6+

Grammar Essentials

Greek grammar can feel complex at first, but these core concepts will get you speaking quickly.

Grammatical Gender

Every Greek noun is masculine (αρσενικό), feminine (θηλυκό), or neuter (ουδέτερο). The article changes to match. Articles also change based on grammatical case (subject, object, etc.).

ο άντρας — the man (masculine)
η γυναίκα — the woman (feminine)
το παιδί — the child (neuter) Definite articles: ο (m), η (f), το (n)

Verb Conjugation — Present Tense

Greek verbs change ending based on person and number. Most verbs follow one of two patterns. The good news: once you learn the pattern, it's consistent.

μιλώ — I speak
μιλάς — you speak
μιλά — he/she speaks
μιλάμε — we speak
μιλάτε — you (plural) speak
μιλάνε — they speak Verb: μιλάω/μιλώ (to speak)

Word Order

Greek word order is relatively flexible (Subject-Verb-Object is most common), but word endings carry the grammatical meaning — so you can rearrange for emphasis without losing clarity.

Ο Γιάννης αγαπά την Μαρία.
Την Μαρία αγαπά ο Γιάννης. Both mean "John loves Maria" — emphasis shifts, meaning doesn't.

Negation

Negating a sentence is simple: place δεν (den) before a verb. For commands and subjunctives, use μην (min) instead.

Μιλώ ελληνικά. — I speak Greek.
Δεν μιλώ ελληνικά. — I don't speak Greek. δεν = don't/doesn't/didn't (with indicative verbs)

Useful Connectors

A handful of small words will let you build longer, more natural sentences from early on.

και (ke) — and  |  ή (i) — or
αλλά (alá) — but  |  γιατί (yatí) — because
όταν (ótan) — when  |  αν (an) — if
όμως (ómos) — however These work the same way as their English equivalents.
